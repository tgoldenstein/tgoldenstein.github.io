THE LOS ANGELES TIMES

Where's my data desk work?

It can be hard to physically show. On the data desk, there is a lot we do that involves helping other reporters in the newsroom. There is also a lot we create that is used as internal tools for reporters. For example, I recently worked on updating an internal arrests database (a Django app) to include a new police agency. This project involved loading emails, scraping HTML files, feeding data into the database, adjusting search queries and automating an email to staffers. There is also a lot we are working on that is still in progress, so stay tuned!

THE ARIZONA REPUBLIC

  • Surprise 91-year-old breaks world bench-press record

Editors often talk about their desire for reporters to go above and beyond the confines of an assignment. This is my best example of doing so. It was a piece for the community section that had been planned as most likely a profile or event coverage on a 91-year-old competitive weightlifter. After speaking to the man and hearing his story, however, I knew we had a lot more there than we expected. Sy's story caught the eye of many, and the article ended up going viral. It was picked up by USA Today, the Associated Press, Drudge Report and more. Jay Leno even mentioned Sy in his monologue that week! The experience was very rewarding, and Sy and I still keep in touch today.

  • Funds for Surprise City Hall draw lawsuit

This piece was part of an investigation I prompted at the end of my internship but ran out of time to continue to pursue. This was a city that had recently announced that it misallocated milllions of dollars and was trying to repay some of that money using a development fee. The fee had been outlawed with the exception that it could be used to repay debt. The city was then challenged in court by a home builders' association over whether money owned to oneself counts as debt. FOIAs that I had sent had not been returned properly before I left.

THE DAILY HERALD

  • Antioch Rescue Squad accused of harassment in lawsuit
  • Antioch Rescue Squad accused in lawsuit of not paying contractor

This was the story that made me realize what a passion I have for investigative journalism. It is still, to this day, one of my proudest and most rewarding reporting moments because it brought on one of the largest investigations the paper did in 2012.

I had been out covering a village board meeting when I learned of a harrassment lawsuit against the local rescue squad that wasn't being covered. I sensed red flags and was given the chance to work with Peter Lisagor Award winner Lee Filas, a senior reporter, on investigating the squad. Despite the limited information we had on their financial activity, we knew even then that money appeared to be missing. We were right.

Our reporting led to city and state involvement, the resignation of top officials and the arrest of one, who pleaded guilty to theft.
